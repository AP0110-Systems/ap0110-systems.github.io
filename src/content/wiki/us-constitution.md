## Summary

Drafted at the Constitutional Convention in 1787, the Constitution is the operating design for the government the Declaration called for. It divides federal power across three branches that check one another, splits authority again between the federal government and the states, makes every rule written and public, and builds in its own amendment process. The first ten amendments — the Bill of Rights — guarantee individual freedoms *against* the system itself: speech, press, assembly, due process, and limits on search and seizure.

## Why it matters

If [the Declaration](/wiki/declaration-of-independence/) is the argument for consent, the Constitution is the engineering that followed: power deliberately distributed so that no single actor controls the whole — [decentralization](/wiki/decentralization/) as constitutional design, two centuries before the term. Its other lesson recurs wherever the rules of shared infrastructure get written: legitimacy comes from public, amendable rules under accountable stewardship, which is exactly the axis on which today's [agent-readable standards diverge](/wiki/governance-of-agent-readable-standards/).
