## 1. SEO & Metadata

### Page Title
Format: `[Org Name] — [Mission Phrase]`

Examples:
- `Hearts Restored — Fighting to End Child Trafficking`
- `Climate Forward — Building a Livable Future`
- `Neighbors United — Affordable Housing for All`

Keep under 60 characters. The mission phrase should mirror hero copy.

### Meta Description
- 1–2 sentences. Mirror the hero subheadline or mission statement.
- Include a soft CTA phrase ("Join us," "Learn how," "See the impact").
- 150–160 characters max.

Example:
> We rescue and restore children from exploitation across four continents. Join us
> in the fight — every child deserves a safe future.

### H1
Should match or closely mirror the hero headline. One per page.

### H2s
Section headers: descriptive and scannable, not clever. Both search engines and
skimmers benefit from literal headers like "How We Work" over "The Journey Forward."

### Image Alt Text
Descriptive and human-centered.
- Bad: `photo1.jpg`, `image of people`, `group shot`
- Good: `Volunteers distributing food in rural Guatemala`, `Students at a community
  literacy workshop in Lagos`

### Canonical URLs
Ensure landing page and about page have canonical tags set correctly. For orgs with
regional or multilingual variants, canonical management is especially important.

## 2. Audience-Segment Tone Calibration

Most landing pages serve multiple visitor types simultaneously. Write primarily for
the **first-time visitor** while ensuring CTAs and trust signals serve donors and partners.

| Visitor Type | What They Need | Tone Adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| **First-time visitor / general public** | Quick orientation: what is this, why does it matter | Accessible, urgent, emotional |
| **Prospective donor** | Trust, impact evidence, ease and safety of giving | Confident, specific, reassuring |
| **Prospective volunteer** | Practical fit, community, personal meaning | Warm, inviting, action-focused |
| **Institutional partner / funder** | Credibility, track record, mission alignment | Professional, factual, partnership-oriented |
| **Media / press** | Clear facts, spokesperson access, story angles | Concise, factual, source-ready |
| **Returning supporter** | Recognition of their role, progress updates, community | Warm, update-focused, celebratory |

### Balancing Multiple Segments on One Page
- **Hero + problem sections:** Calibrate for first-time visitors. Assume no prior knowledge.
- **Proof / scale section:** Calibrate for donors and institutional partners.
- **CTA section:** Calibrate for whoever is most valuable to convert (usually donors
  or volunteers).
- **Testimonials:** Most effective for first-time visitors and prospective donors.
- **News / updates section:** Most valued by returning supporters.

## 3. Testimonials & Sensitive Populations

### General Best Practices
- Quotes from real beneficiaries, volunteers, partners, and longtime supporters.
- One powerful, specific quote outperforms several generic ones.
- Include first name and context for credibility: role, location, or year.
  - Example: *"— Maria, volunteer since 2019, São Paulo"*
  - Example: *"— James K., member, Chicago chapter"*

### Sensitive Populations
For orgs serving survivors, minors, refugees, people in crisis, or any population
with safety or privacy concerns:

- **Do not use full names** of beneficiaries without explicit, documented consent.
- **Paraphrased accounts** are appropriate: *"One young woman we work with described
  the shelter as the first place she felt safe."*
- **Composite stories** are acceptable when clearly disclosed: *"The following story
  reflects the experiences of many of the young people we serve."*
- **Minors:** Never use identifying photos or names without parental/guardian consent.
  Describe ages in ranges if needed ("a girl, about 10 years old").
- **Do not use imagery that could re-traumatize** the people depicted or the viewer.
  Dignified, hopeful imagery outperforms graphic or distressing depictions in both
  ethics and conversion.

### Quotes from Institutional Voices
Partners, funders, government officials, and community leaders can be quoted by
full name and title. Always verify accuracy and obtain approval before publishing.

## 4. Footer Content Requirements

Every page footer should include:

### Required
- **Legal name** of the organization as registered
- **Tax-exempt status** (e.g., "A registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit corporation")
- **Federal tax ID / EIN** (U.S. orgs): "Federal ID # XX-XXXXXXX"
- **Tax deductibility notice** (U.S. orgs): "All donations are tax-deductible to the
  full extent permitted by law."
- **Physical address** or headquarters location
- **Primary contact** (email or phone)
- **Social media links**

### Required Navigation Links
- About
- Programs / What We Do
- Donate / Give
- Contact
- Privacy Policy
- Terms of Use

### Recommended
- Link to annual report or financials page
- Newsletter signup
- Charity watchdog ratings (Charity Navigator, GiveWell, etc.) if strong
- Language selector (for orgs with multilingual audiences)

### For International Orgs
- Note regional office locations briefly (country + city)
- Clarify which entity accepts donations in which jurisdiction if relevant

## 5. Extended Headline Formulas

Use these when crafting hero headlines. The best headlines are specific to the org's
cause and tone — these are starting frameworks, not fill-in-the-blank templates.

### Commitment-to-the-cause formula
*"We will [do / fight / build / not stop] until [outcome]."*
- Direct, mission-first, personal conviction. Works best for direct-service orgs.

### Stakes + identity formula
*"The world needs [X]. [X] needs you."*
- Positions the visitor as essential. Works well for advocacy and membership orgs.

### Vision-forward formula
*"[The world we're building]: [the values that define it]."*
- Aspirational. Works well for rights-based or systems-change orgs.

### Problem-meets-promise formula
*"[Scale of problem]. We won't accept it."*
- Acknowledges reality and declares defiance. Strong for urgent or crisis-focused orgs.

### Movement identity formula
*"A movement of [who] standing up for [what]."*
- Best for membership orgs or orgs with a clear constituent identity.

### Subheadline note
The subheadline always expands or grounds the headline — it should add information,
not restate the headline in different words. It should include at least one of:
who is affected, what the org does, or where the work happens.

## 6. News & Updates Section

### Purpose
Shows the organization is active and current. Builds trust with returning visitors and
gives search engines fresh content signals.

### Format
- 3 items max on the landing page (link to full blog/news archive)
- Include: headline, date, 1-sentence summary, "Read more" link
- Most recent first
- Acceptable content types: blog posts, press coverage, campaign updates, impact
  stories, event announcements

### What to Avoid
- Items older than 12 months (unless genuinely landmark content)
- Press releases in full — link out, summarize inline
- Overly internal news ("We attended a conference") unless it demonstrates mission impact

## 7. Values Section Guidance

### When to Include
Values sections work best when:
- The org has a distinctive ethical or philosophical stance worth naming
- The org operates in complex territory (multiple countries, sensitive populations)
  where values help explain decisions
- The org wants to attract mission-aligned staff, volunteers, or partners

They are less valuable when they're generic ("Integrity. Excellence. Community.")
and add no differentiation.

### How to Write Values
- 3–5 values, named in 1–3 words each
- Follow each with 2–4 sentences that make the value concrete and specific to the org
- Demonstrate the value in the copy itself — don't just claim it

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- "Integrity" as a value with no explanation of what that means in practice
- Values that any org in any sector could claim ("Excellence," "Innovation")
- Lists of 8+ values — this signals that none of them are actually priorities

## 8. Leadership & Team Section Guidance

### When to Lead with Leadership
- When founder credibility is central to trust (e.g., a recognized expert or
  activist as founder)
- When the org's personality is strongly tied to specific individuals
- When recruiting is a primary goal of the about page

### When to Lead with Community
- Membership orgs, grassroots movements, or advocacy networks
- Orgs where the constituency or beneficiaries are the story
- Cases where an individual-focused narrative might undermine collective ownership

### Bio Format
- Name + title
- 2–4 sentences: relevant experience, personal connection to the mission
- Photo (humanizes; include if possible)
- Keep bios for leadership page; about page bios should be brief

## 9. Multi-Page Site Architecture Notes

A complete nonprofit website typically includes:

| Page | Primary Purpose |
|---|---|
| Home / Landing | Mission orientation, emotional hook, primary CTAs |
| About | Trust, history, values, team, transparency |
| Programs / What We Do | Detailed explanation of work by program area |
| Get Involved | Donation, volunteering, membership, advocacy paths |
| Impact / Stories | Proof of work — case studies, data, testimonials |
| News / Blog | Freshness signal, thought leadership, campaign updates |
| Contact | Accessibility, responsiveness signal |
| Annual Report / Financials | Donor and partner transparency |
| FAQs | Objection handling, trust building |

The landing page and about page are the **highest-priority pages** for most visitors —
they are the foundation. Other pages deepen or convert interest that those pages establish.

When writing copy for any page beyond landing and about, apply the same core
principles (cause-first, active voice, concrete stakes, clear CTAs) with content
adapted to the specific page's purpose.
